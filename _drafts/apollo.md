The company distributed 7000 tonnes of fertilizers to more than 100,000 farmers in November last year. 
The Africa Fertilizer Financing Mechanism provided a $2 million partial trade credit guarantee and a $219,000 grant to Apollo Agriculture to sustain the input needs of agro-dealers, retail shops, and farmers within their digital platform. The deal will run between 2024 to 2026. 
Apollo Agriculture employs data science and software technology to create farmer profiles. Information obtained can be used to determine loan utilization and repayment time-frame. 
The company’s goal in achieving food security involves empowering subsistence farmers to become large-scale profitable producers.

- At The Climate Corporation he was using these AI / ML models to help big Agra improve their yields by ~5%
- Now the models are mainly for fraud detection. It’s harder to optimize the underwriting part as well. They’re all high credit risk anyways basically (just virtue of them dirt poor with no buffer)
- Started with a colleague form the company and then a friend who knew the African business market and stuff
- Its a Kenyan company to Kenyans, a Zambian company to Zambians, and an American company to SF VCs.
- There is competition, mainly from NGOs
- Useful to have more experience. Easier to have an impact. Rn is a period of tremendous growth for me and joining the job market would mean I lose out on this. Like Christina said, what’s something that genuinely can’t wait 4 years of my life to do?
- 
- He wants to create lasting trust and change in the farmers, to help their lives in the long term
- Kenyan farmers make 10~20%/acre compared to their american peers. So it’s easy to have 2x,3x results here.
- The financing is very important as it allows them to access equipment and materials they otherwise wouldn’t be able to afford at all.
- The reason that no one else has taken the market is because people think it’s hard. No one wants to take the step and come to Nairobi to do this. No bank here is willing to lend to small farmers. 
- On the one hand making money off of some of the poorest people is obviously hard, but when you realize how much room there is for them to grow its suddenly not that bad of an idea at all
- There’s less sharks in the tank because few people believed
- When he first started out, it was scary and daunting as there was n feedback form the universe about it. He didn’t know if he was doing the right thing until more than a year in. A point in his life where choosing one path over the other shut down doors and windows. 
- It was a big leap of faith for him and he was excited but also not sure of the future.
- It’s easier to learn from others as an employee than when you’re a CEO
- They have agents go around recruiting people. They earn based on num of ppl they get and size and other stuff.
- Agents distributed equally where farming is a good idea
- For places like in north east, thats where we just need philanthropy. It’s not for a company like Apollo. 
- Enabled now by technology of smart phones
- They’re really an e-commerce and that’s ok. Don’t need to reinvent just need to deliver
- A bit like uber, control their agents through the app
- Send out training to the farmers through short audio training clips since they only have mobile phones (3/4)
- Currently 200,000 farmers on board in Kenya, 10,000 in Zambia
- Can’t expand because of capital constraints — team bandwidth and capital
- Tanzania would be easier but Thailand harder
- Amsterdam because its closer timezone wise but not everyone wants to move to Nairobi
- The goal is to move the farmers away from substance farming to farming as a business
- Farmers are more conservative, which if you think about it is pretty rationally since they get maybe 50 paychecks in their lifetime. 1~2 seasons per year. This is a risk of death if their once 6 month paycheck fails
- Planting tomatoes is big risk big reward whereas Maize is safe because its what they eat anyways (ugali)
